When it comes to the brain,
mild stress such as a deadline,
can improve cognitive
performance since we tend to
put our full attention on the
task at hand. However,
intense and long-term stress
has the opposite effect: the
brain triggers the adrenal
glands to release stress
hormones like cortisol and
adrenaline, damaging nerve
cells in the hippocampus and
impairing memory. To
counteract such stress, BCBS
offers a specific health
coaching experience geared
toward stress management.

March Theme: Brain Awareness
Helping Celebrate National Brain Awareness Week!
Brain Awareness Week is the
global campaign to increase
public awareness of the
progress and benefits of brain
research. Every March it is
coordinated by the Dana
Alliance for Brain Initiatives, a
nonprofit organization
committed to accessible
education about the brain. This
years Brain Awareness Week
falls on Monday 3/12 Sunday
3/18!
In the spirit of awareness, the
Alliance has also put together a
collaborative guide to cognitive
fitness. The term refers to a
state in which we are
performing well mentally,
emotionally, and functionally.
Modern neuroscience indicates
that we have some control over
maintaining our mental acuity.
The key is to keep our brains
active what we do everyday
matters to our brains! Check
out these tips on how to
maintain your mental edge:
Use it or lose it! Just as
we need exercise to
maintain our physical
fitness, our brains need
constant cognitive
stimulation to ensure our
brain health! Mental
activity increases synaptic
connections and brain
flexibility. The more we
put the brain to work, the
better it is at finding new
neural pathways to adapt
to changes. Studies
support that information
processing activities like
reading newspapers or
solving puzzles decreases
the risk of Alzheimers
disease. And more
research has shown that
cognitive training
programs have boosted
mental functioning in

When you work your
body, your brain
benefits! There is much
scientific evidence that
physical activity has
numerous brain benefits
including: enhancing
memory and learning,
improving mood and
counteracting depression,
increasing blood and
oxygen levels via
enlarged blood vessels,
increasing a growth factor
that supports and
nourishes brain cells,
increasing brain cells that
support neurons and
speed neural processing
Meet, greet, and be
social! Many studies
prove the value of social
interaction in cognitive
health. It makes sense
that when we interact
with others, we are
actively using our brains!
Because people are
unpredictable, our brains
must be alert during our
interactions. It also
appears that having
strong social networks
can help with stress
management and
improve our sense of self-
worth which is important
considering the emotional
component of cognitive
fitness. Moreover,
instances of social
isolation have been tied to
health issues and shorter
lifespans.
Feed your brain! What
we eat matters to our
brains. While dietary
advice constantly shifts
and contradicts itself,
there are certain trusty
foods that are
unquestionably healthy
for our brains and bodies:
Leafy greens and
cruciferous vegetables
(cauliflower, broccoli,
cabbage) which improve
cognition; Fish
containing omega-3 fatty
acids essential for brain
function (salmon, tuna,
mackerel); Whole grains
also related to
cardiovascular health;
Blueberries, walnuts,
sunflower seeds,
pomegranates, legumes
and other antioxidant-
rich foods that help fight
free radicals that
damage the body; And
yes even red wine in
moderation can benefit
the brain!
Sleep and rest well!
Getting restful sleep each
night helps with memory
consolidation and
retaining information.
